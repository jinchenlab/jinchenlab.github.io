We're interested in how cellular identity is set, maintained, and — most importantly — changed. The four directions below are independent enough to pursue in parallel and tightly coupled enough that progress in one usually propels the others.

## Rejuvenation and disease reversal

Aging and many diseases share a common signature: cells exist in states that they could occupy when young and healthy, but no longer return to. We ask whether those return trips are possible. What is the minimum perturbation — chemical, genetic, or environmental — that restores a cell to a younger, more functional state? Which deviations are reversible, which are not, and why? Our goal is to convert the growing catalog of aging and disease signatures into actionable interventions.

## Cell states in development and disease

Cell *state* — not just cell *type* — is the level at which biology operates. The same cell can occupy radically different states depending on stress, age, signaling, and history, and most diseases involve states that exist transiently in healthy tissue but become locked in at the wrong time or place. We're building a quantitative grammar of cell states: how they are defined, how they transition, which transitions are reversible, and which microproteins, RNAs, and signaling inputs control them.

## The dark proteome — microproteins as state regulators

The human genome encodes thousands of small proteins translated from regions long considered non-coding. Earlier work from the lab and others has shown that many of these microproteins are real, regulated, and functional. Yet most remain uncharacterized — a dark proteome hiding in plain sight. We're asking systematically which microproteins control cell-state transitions, with a particular focus on those that act as bottlenecks during aging, regeneration, and dedifferentiation.

## High-throughput technology development

Answering the questions above requires reading and writing cell state at scale. We develop sequencing, perturbation, and imaging methods — building on our background in functional genomics, ribosome profiling, Perturb-seq, and CRISPR-based epigenetic editing — that let us interrogate microproteins, translation, and state transitions in millions of cells in parallel. New technology is not an end in itself for us; it's how we expand what biology can ask.
